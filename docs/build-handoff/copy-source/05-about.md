## S2 · The story

*Layout: editorial prose, ~75ch measure; two pull-quotes set in the drafted style; dates as margin numerals.*

**2015.** A Corner Bakery in Pikesville, Maryland. Justin Shaw was working a job he didn't want, and a friend asked him the question that started everything: *"What do you like?"* The honest answer — "suits, I guess" — took two more years to become anything.

**2017.** It became something at a desk, with a prayer, when a $1,000 grant call came minutes after he'd decided to risk it. He gave himself ninety days. On September 1 at 7 p.m., his custom clothing company went live; by midnight it was booked out two weeks, and by October he'd quit the day job. For years he drove rideshare from 5 a.m. to noon and built in the evenings.

**The decade since.** That company grew into a six-figure business that dressed Baltimore's mayor twice. Justin graduated from Goldman Sachs' 10,000 Small Businesses program, co-authored a book, hosted more than a hundred events for entrepreneurs, and generated over a million dollars across his ventures — most of that time spent doing one thing in different costumes: putting resources in founders' hands. `[STATS — his self-authored receipts; confirm the set — README #5/#7]`

**Pull-quote (press-verified, attributed):** "It's not a transaction for me, it's a transformation." — *Justin Shaw, AFRO, 2023*

**The pattern.** Everywhere he worked, the same thing kept being true: the resources founders need mostly exist. The grants, the advisors, the rooms — they're out there. They're just camouflaged, findable only by people who already know somebody. Programs end. Cohorts graduate. And founders go back to building alone.

**2026.** Ventriq is his answer — a nonprofit that holds capital, programming, and council under one roof, so no founder has to choose which kind of help to go hunting for by themselves. It's the structure he wishes had existed at that desk in 2017.
