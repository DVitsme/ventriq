## Lane 1 · Maryland charitable solicitation registration — THE blocker

**The rule (verified Jul 2026):** Under the Maryland Solicitations Act (Bus. Reg. Title 6), a charitable organization must be on file with the **MD Secretary of State BEFORE any solicitation begins** — and a Donate page going live *is* the solicitation; no dollar needs to arrive. Two tracks: expecting **under $25,000/yr in charitable contributions** and using no paid fundraiser → the free **Exempt Organization Fund-Raising Notice** (short form, filed via MD OneStop, renewed annually within 8 months of FY end); **$25,000+ or a paid solicitor** → full **COR-92 registration** (sliding fee $50–$300 by contribution level, attachments: articles, bylaws, officer list, Form 990, and *IRS determination letter — or a copy of Form 1023 if exemption is pending*, per SOS practice). Processing: **10–45 days**. Soliciting unregistered: fines **up to $5,000 per violation** plus $25/month late fees. A pending-501(c)(3) org **can** file — MD accepts the 1023 copy in lieu of the letter (confirm the Notice-level attachment with the SOS/counsel; the Notice form itself is minimal and free).

**Blocks:** YES — the Aug 1 Donate page, the Aug 10 "donate during the summit" moment, **and** Eventbrite's donation add-on (Q8) are all gated on this filing. Filed this week, the timeline holds; discovered in August, it doesn't. Note: mastermind fees are program revenue, not "charitable contributions," so Ventriq very likely starts on the free Notice track — upgrade to COR-92 the year contributions hit $25k.

**Internet donations from other states:** the **Charleston Principles** (NASCO, 2001 — non-binding guidance most of the ~41 registration states informally follow) say a *passive* donate page only triggers another state's registration if you target that state or receive repeated/substantial contributions from it. Practical norm for a new org: register at home (MD), keep appeals un-targeted, monitor donor geography.

## Lane 2 · Donation mechanics — receipts, pending status, the 27-month rule

**The rule:** IRS Pub 1771: donors need a **contemporaneous written acknowledgment for any single gift of $250+** (org name, amount, date, and a statement that no goods or services were provided — or a description and good-faith value if they were). A **quid-pro-quo** payment **over $75** (part gift, part goods/services) requires a written disclosure; penalty **$10 per contribution, up to $5,000 per event/mailing**. Pre-determination: the IRS states donors have **no advance assurance of deductibility while the application is pending** — if exemption is ultimately granted, contributions made in the covered period **are** deductible; if denied, they aren't. **27-month rule:** file Form 1023/1023-EZ within 27 months of formation and (if approved) exemption — and deductibility — is **retroactive to the formation date**, covering every summit-week donation.

**Blocks:** soft-blocks Donate copy and the Stripe receipt template. Never print "tax-deductible" unqualified (Q2 already says this); the receipt needs the approved pending-status sentence before the first dollar arrives.

- [ ] **70. (→A, 🔴) Form 1023 — submitted, or only planned?** Sharpening Q2: confirm the 1023/1023-EZ is actually **filed** and send the submission confirmation + formation date. Filed within 27 months of formation and later approved, every donation back to formation day becomes retroactively deductible — and the MD registration wants the 1023 copy too. *Why: one date determines what the Donate page may promise.*
- [ ] **71. (→F, 🟠) Donation receipt template — approve the exact language.** Auto-receipt on every Stripe gift: name, amount, date, "no goods or services were provided," plus, until determination: *"Ventriq has applied for recognition as a tax-exempt 501(c)(3) organization; if granted, contributions are expected to be deductible retroactive to [formation date]. Consult your tax advisor."* And confirm donors receive **nothing** in return (no merch/perks/event admission) — otherwise quid-pro-quo disclosures apply above $75. Counsel skims the template once. *Why: IRS Pub 1771 — $250+ gifts need this in writing, and missing quid-pro-quo language is $10/donation in penalties.*

## Lane 3 · The paid mastermind under a nonprofit

**Orientation (for the accountant, not a launch gate):** fees for a program that *is* the charitable mission — education/training for founders — are **related program-service revenue**, not unrelated business income; UBIT (IRC §§511–513) taxes only a trade or business *regularly carried on* that is *not substantially related* to the exempt purpose. A mission-core mastermind is normally related even if profitable, but scale/commerciality and any private-benefit optics belong on the accountant's and counsel's desk at 990 time. **Consumer side:** Maryland's Consumer Protection Act (Com. Law Title 13) requires material terms — **including refund policy — disclosed before purchase**; MD's 3-day cooling-off applies to door-to-door/off-premises sales, not a web application flow, but the disclosure duty is real. At $2.5–7.5k, the written policy is also the entire Stripe-chargeback defense.

- [ ] **73. (→C, 🟠) Mastermind refund & cancellation terms — pick them before applications open.** What happens when an accepted founder pays and quits, or is removed? Common patterns: deposit refundable until cohort start / 7-day exit window after kickoff / no refunds but one transfer to a later cohort. We draft the policy page + checkout acknowledgment; counsel reviews; accountant separately confirms program-revenue (non-UBIT) treatment. *Why: MD law wants terms disclosed pre-purchase, and a $5k charge with no written refund terms is a chargeback that wins.*

## Lane 4 · Speaker agreements for the summit

**The rule/practice:** Maryland is an **all-party consent** state (Cts. & Jud. Proc. §10-402) — recording a session without every participant's consent is a **felony** (up to 5 yrs / $10,000), so recording consent is signed paper, not vibes; attendees get a recording notice at Zoom registration. A minimal speaker release covers: (1) consent to record; (2) a **perpetual, non-exclusive license** to host, edit, and redistribute the replay — expressly including the gated members community and promo clips; (3) **name/likeness/bio/headshot** for the speaker wall, agenda, and promo kit; (4) speaker warrants the content/slides are theirs; (5) any compensation or **affiliate links** stated (FTC Endorsement Guides, 16 CFR 255, require disclosing material connections when speakers promote for a benefit); (6) independent-contractor/no-endorsement boilerplate.

**Blocks:** YES — the 24-hour-replay promise (Q10) and the whole replays-gated-in-Skool funnel (Q50) are worthless without signed licenses, and Aug 10 recording without consent is legal exposure, not just bad form.

## Lane 5 · In-person events (Founders After Hours, Baltimore)

**The practice:** venues routinely require a **certificate of insurance — typically $1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate general liability, venue named additional insured**; a new nonprofit either buys an annual GL policy or single-night event policies (~$100–200/event). Host-liquor: n/a per plan (confirm no self-poured alcohol — that changes the answer). Photos: US norm for adult attendees is **notice at RSVP + signage at the door** ("attending = consent to event photography"); written releases only for people featured in close-up marketing; minors need guardian consent. **Permits:** Baltimore City special-event permits target public property, streets/parks, tents, amplified outdoor sound — a private gathering inside a licensed private venue generally needs **no city permit** (the venue's occupancy and licenses carry it); verify per-venue, and note Baltimore *County* has its own gathering-permit regime if FAH ever moves.

## Lane 6 · Communications — CAN-SPAM and SMS/TCPA

**The rule:** **CAN-SPAM** (FTC guide) applies to nonprofits' commercial email: truthful headers/subjects, a clear opt-out honored within **10 business days**, and a **valid physical postal address in every marketing email** — street address, USPS PO Box, or registered commercial mailbox. **TCPA (current state, 2025–26):** the FCC's one-to-one consent rule was **vacated** (11th Cir., *IMC v. FCC*, Jan 2025; FCC formally deleted it Sept 2025) — the pre-2023 standard governs: **prior express written consent** for marketing texts; plain **prior express consent** (knowingly providing the number for that purpose) suffices for purely informational reminders. Since **Apr 11, 2025**: revocation by **any reasonable means**, honored within **10 business days**; a revoke can kill both text types. Practice regardless: unchecked consent checkbox + disclosure at registration, STOP/HELP in every message — and **carrier 10DLC campaign registration takes ~2–3 weeks**, a scheduling fact, not a legal one.
